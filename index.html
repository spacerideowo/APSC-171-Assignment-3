Aerial Refueling Simulation

This website explains what aerial refueling is, why it is important, how it connects to physics concepts such as relative velocity, and why real-life aerial refueling is much harder than a simple classroom model.

Part One

Introduction

Aerial refueling is a vital part of modern air force operations. Aerial refueling is the process in which a receiver aircraft, which is low on fuel, is connected to a tanker aircraft, and fuel is transferred from the tanker to the receiver. For this to properly occur, both aircraft must be flying at the same speed and in the same direction in very close proximity. There are two commonly used methods for connecting the aircraft so that fuel can be transferred. The first is the drogue-and-probe method, which involves a basket-like drogue at the end of a flexible fuel hose that hangs from the tanker. The receiving aircraft has a refueling probe (usually attached near the nose) which connects to the drogue when the pilot flies into it. The second method for in-air refueling is the boom method, in which a rigid boom from the tanker is lowered and directed toward the receiver aircraft by an operator in the rear of the tanker. The drogue-and-probe method is more common, since it is easier to use and less expensive; however, it can only be used for smaller aircraft due to a slow fuel-transfer rate.

Our group selected aerial refueling as our topic because of its extensive application of the concept of relative motion. In order for aerial refueling to work, pilots in both planes involved must fly in such a way that their relative velocity is very near zero; otherwise, they cannot stay connected to transfer any fuel. A very low relative velocity is also important due to the close proximity that the aircraft need to fly to each other to facilitate connection between them, and there is a risk of collision involved in any sudden changes in speed or direction.

As mentioned before, aerial refueling is a vital part of modern air force operations. In-air refueling allows aircraft to fly much greater distances and stay in the air for much longer. This is especially useful in the navy, in which aircraft may be taking off from an aircraft carrier or are far away from an airbase. Its value is also apparent in longer-lasting missions or operations, which may not allow for aircraft to fly long distances and use the extra time to return to base.

Part Two

Core Physics Concepts

Connection to Course Concepts

Aerial refueling can be analyzed using concepts from dynamics, especially relative motion and Newton’s second law. In this process, the receiver aircraft must move in a controlled way relative to the tanker aircraft in order to maintain a stable refueling position.

One of the most important ideas is relative motion. Research on aerial refueling systems shows that the relative motion between the receiver and the tanker is a key part of the problem. In course terms, this connects directly to L09 Relative Motion, where motion is described using relative position, relative velocity, and relative acceleration.

rrel, vrel, arel

To refuel successfully, the relative position between the two aircraft must remain approximately constant. This means the receiver and tanker must travel at nearly the same speed and in the same direction. A useful relationship is:

Δx = vrel · t

According to this equation, even a small difference in velocity can cause the separation distance to increase over time. Ideally, the relative velocity should be close to 0 m/s so that the receiver does not drift away from the tanker.

Another important concept is Newton’s second law, which connects forces to motion:

ΣF = ma

In real flight conditions, the aircraft are affected by external forces such as wind, turbulence, airflow, and drag. These forces produce changes in acceleration, which then affect velocity and position. As a result, the relative motion between the two aircraft is constantly changing and must be corrected continuously during refueling.

Real aerial refueling is even more difficult because the hose-and-drogue system must remain stable. If the receiver moves too fast or the airflow changes too much, the hose can lose proper tension and become slack, which increases the risk of instability and makes refueling harder.

Why This Matters in Our Simulation

Our simulation focuses mainly on relative velocity and gap closing, which helps demonstrate the core physics idea in a simple way. However, real aerial refueling is more complex because it also involves external disturbances, drag effects, and stability of the hose system.

Part Four

Real-World Challenges

As beneficial as aerial refueling is, there are a significant number of challenges associated with this practice. The difficulty in properly aligning the tanker to the aircraft, as well as the numerous large accidents due to small midair mistakes, are the main concerns with aerial refueling. Aerial refueling requires so much precision; otherwise, it could be extremely dangerous.

Aerial refueling is considered exceptionally difficult because the two aircraft involved in this process have to be incredibly stable at tremendously fast speeds, usually around 200 knots. Not only this, but they also have to maintain a mere 47 feet to avoid crashing into one another (Clark, 2025). The tanker and the receiving aircraft have to remain in constant contact with one another for this task to be successfully completed. Turbulence, crosswinds, and slipstream effects are created in the aircraft's wake, putting huge amounts of force on the plane and making proper connection between the two vessels a very grueling mission. Between 200 and 450 lbf of drag is placed on the receiver aircraft during aerial refueling (Vachon & Ray, 2004). Aerial refueling is a highly specialized process that requires precise execution, careful planning, and extensive training.

Multiple instances have occurred where what seemed like minor mistakes cost billions' worth of damage, proving how high the stakes are during aerial refueling and how difficult the job is. In 2024, the boom was connected to an F-15E’s fuel port; however, the fighter continued its flight path towards the tanker a little too long and compressed the boom to 3.75 feet—two feet shorter than its normal operation. When the two aircraft attempted to separate, the boom got stuck in the fuel port and got pulled on by the aircraft with 7,404 pounds of force. This caused the boom to spring violently upwards with extreme force, smashing into the tail section, then oscillating in an uncontrollable manner until finally breaking off and dropping into a remote area in a California forest. Damage repairs for this instance cost $14,381,303 (White, 2025). There have been more examples of occurrences such as this, with probes and booms getting stuck in the receiving aircraft due to small mistakes. These accidents were most likely due to the boom operators while connecting the aircraft, errors from the pilot while flying, and/or weaknesses in the design of the controls that are used to operate the boom. That being said, according to M. White, the accident board found that the “boom operator's actions were not intentional or preventable, and [the mishap] was due to a poorly designed flight control system.”

Aerial refueling is a very complex and precise maneuver for tankers and aircraft to perform, and mistakes could result in catastrophic collisions.
